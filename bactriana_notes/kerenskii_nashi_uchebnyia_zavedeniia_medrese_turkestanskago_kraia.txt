## O. Kerenskii, “Nashi uchebnyia zavedeniia: Medrese Turkestanskago kraia,” in Zhurnal" ministerstva narodnago prosveshcheniia No. 11, Turkestanskii Sbornik“: Stat’i i zametki iz” russkikh“ i inostrannykh” razet" 418 (Tashkent, 1892).

For backstory on Kerenskii, see [[Argutinskii (1909) l polozhenii narodnoro obrazovaniia v Turkestanom krae i o deiatel'nosti F. Kerenskoro (RGIA F1396 O1 D337)]].

*Note: first page number is from the Turkestanskii Sbornik, the second is from the article iteslf*

- Article based on two sources of data: Government data and the observations of private observers (1/18).
- Traces the foundatin of the majority of madrasas in Samarkand, Kokand, and Tashkent to the 15th century (1/18).
- Physical features of the madrasa: "... худжра, или кельи для учащихся (муллы), а иногда и для учителей (мударрисы), дарсъ-хане, то-есть, классная комната, одна или нѣсколько, и, наконецъ, мечеть" (1/18).
- Average size of the sells was "3 до 6 кв. аршинъ" (arshin = 0.71 m) which held 3-4 mullas (1/18).
- Kerenskii's description of life in the cells is one of gloom and cold: "Въ кельяхъ почти всегда тѣсно и мрачно, а зимой холодно и сыро" (2/19).
- Every madrassa has a mosque between the cells and classrooms (2/19).
- The outward appearance of madrassas in Bukhara and Khiva are the same as those in Turkestan.
- Author notes that small madrassas are of a different type than those he described; i.e. small wooden ones with external mosques, etc (2/19).
- Most madrassas are to be found in large cities and bazaar towns (базарныхъ селенiяхъ).  They are usually located at the center of the town near the bazaar (2/19).
- At this point Kerensky gets into a discussion of __waqf__.  He notes that all waqfs are establihsed by private indviduals.  While many were founded by the emirs, they acted in the capacity of private individuals, not as from the state "Медресе учреждались на ихъ личныя средства, а не на государственныя" (3/20).
- According to Kerensky, Kokand enjoys pride of place in terms of quality of madrassas and waqf revenue (though he is probably talking about Turkestan): Наилучшими зданіями и наибольшими вакуфными доходами обладаютъ мадресе в г. Коканда. Здѣсь на 34 медресе приходится годоваго дохода свыше 64 тысячъ (3/20).
- Kerensky notes the presence in many madrassas of "holy hair" from the Prophet's beard, i.e. mui-mubarak.  In Tashkent he notes that in 1857 the Hakim Mirza-Akhmat built a madrassa that is known known as the Medrese-i Mu-yi Mubarak (3/20).
- Has some discussion of the legal procedure for when a waqf falls into disrepair or becomes unprofitable and needs to be entrusted to someone else (4/21).
- Kerensky notes the various sorts of trusts that can be set up (including cash and slaves, which he notes are rare), as well as the possibility of books: Вакуфныя библіотеки, въ количествѣ нѣсколькихъ соть томовъ книгъ, были только при двухъ медресе, но теперь и ихъ нетъ; всѣ книги растеряны. Мударрисы и муллы, оставляя медресе, уносили книги съ собой, а мутаваліи, обязанные охранять вакуфное имущество, не обращали никакого вниманія на библіотеки медресе (4/21).
